There was a guy dying in an alley in Seattle, and I was the only one who did anything about it.

Two hotel employees were standing over him — a man splayed flat on his back on the pavement, completely unresponsive — and both of them were on separate phone calls to 911. Neither one was touching him. People were walking past on the street, glancing down the alley for half a second, and moving on.

That made me angrier than I've been in a long time.

I went over, got on my knees, and started chest compressions. I asked the hotel employees how long he'd been like this, whether anyone had seen him fall. I didn't know it at the time, but I was asking the right questions. I just had no idea what to do with the answers.

The man was in opioid overdose, at least I suspected as much. It was Seattle, after all. In retrospect, I was probably correct. I remember his pupils were pinpoint, though I didn't know yet what that meant. However, I didn't know that opioids suppress respiration. I also didn't know how to do a head tilt-chin lift to open his airway or how to provide artificial ventilations. I knew one thing: chest compressions. So I did that for three or four minutes until EMS arrived.

I'm pretty sure that man was already dead. He was completely limp in a way you can't fake. There was no pressure left in his lungs. When I did compressions, his ribs flexed like old rubber. It was haunting. I don't know what happened to him after the ambulance took him away, and I think about it.

But I know what that moment did to me.

I've been in computer science since I was 16, when I built my first website. I spent years doing freelance web design for marketing companies, eventually starting my own business doing social media content for contractors and construction companies. I was good at it. I made good money. I was in control of my time and my life in a way most people don't get to be.

And I realized, standing in that alley, that none of it was enough.

That's not a criticism of the work. The problems I was solving are real. A company with a broken conversion funnel loses money. A contractor with no social presence loses clients to someone who has one. Those things are worth fixing.

But a man dying in an alley, surrounded by people who don't know what to do, is a different category of problem. It's the kind I cannot tolerate being unable to solve.

So at 29 years old, I'm walking away from a decade in tech to become an EMT, and eventually a physician assistant. That means giving up at least part of the business, giving up most of the income, giving up the identity of someone who built something from scratch and knew exactly what he was doing. It means being an entry-level grunt for a while. It means going back to school at 30.

Nobody who knows me was surprised when I told them. Not my parents, not my close friends. They didn't see it coming, but they all said some version of: *"Yeah — that makes sense."* That was more validating than I expected. There's something specific about feeling pulled toward something and hearing from the people who know you best that the pull is real.

The hardest thing to walk away from isn't the money. It's the competence. There's a particular comfort in mastering a domain. In knowing the answers, in being respected for your expertise, in having built a reputation around being useful. I'm stepping back into beginner territory, which is uncomfortable by definition.

But I've already started. And it's already worth it.
