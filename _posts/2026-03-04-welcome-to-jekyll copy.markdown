Here's what I got wrong going in: I assumed that if I knew the material, I'd be able to use it.
That assumption lasted about thirty minutes into my first simulation.

I'm good at learning (when I want to be). Always have been — flashcards, mnemonics, pattern recognition across complex systems. So I walked into EMT training expecting the knowledge to be the wall. All that anatomy, those protocols, the acronyms and triads and drug interactions. I figured that was where people washed out.

Turns out I had it exactly backwards. Give me a week and I'll know Beck's Triad, Cushing's Triad, and the most common cause of an epidural hematoma. That part was fine. What wasn't fine was what happened when I had to act on it with a simulated patient in front of me and a clock running.
Knowing and doing, it turns out, live in completely different parts of your brain.

I'm a cerebral person. I live in my head. And it turns out that when you put me in front of a simulated patient — a real human being, or a very convincing actor with stage blood on their forehead — all that organized, well-indexed information becomes suddenly difficult to work with. The stress response is real. My brain knows what the signs of increased intracranial pressure are. My brain, under pressure, with a patient in front of me and a clock running, sometimes forgets what they look like.

That's what I'm working on now. It's not a knowledge problem. It's a performance-under-pressure problem, and it turns out those are completely different animals.

The program I did was a two-week intensive — 12 hours a day at a school in Arizona. You can spread it out over three months at most community colleges if you prefer, but the crash course suited me. I take my NREMT certification exam next week and expect to pass. The knowledge component, again, is fine.

What made the program genuinely great — and I wasn't expecting this — was the simulation training. Real actors. Makeup. Fake blood and staged scenarios designed to put your nervous system under load and see what comes out. I walked in expecting a classroom. I got something closer to live-action training with real emotional stakes attached to it. There's something about a "patient" with tears running down their face and a convincing wound on their arm that reaches your nervous system differently than a textbook question ever will. You can study shock all you want. It hits differently when someone is performing it in front of you and waiting for you to respond correctly.

That gap — between knowing and doing — is exactly what the simulations are designed to close. I have a lot of respect for how the course was built.

I also thought I'd be the old guy. I'm 29, and I walked in half-expecting to be surrounded by 18-year-olds and feel accordingly out of place. There were people in their late 30s and early 40s. Most of the class was 23–25, a lot of them firefighters building out their certifications. No one made me feel strange about my age. Everyone was there for the same reason: they wanted to be more useful when something goes wrong. That shared purpose creates a specific kind of bond quickly, and the friendships that came out of those two weeks were something I didn't anticipate.

I'm six months to a year away from having the patient care hours I need to apply to PA school. EMT pay is not impressive. But I'm not doing this for the money — I'm doing this because a man in a Seattle alley needed someone who knew more than I did, and that is not a feeling I'm willing to carry into the rest of my life.

Learning how to not be useless in a crisis is, it turns out, worth quite a bit.
